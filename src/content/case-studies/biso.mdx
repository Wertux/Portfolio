This was my first professional project — and one that threw me in at the deep end. As both Product Owner and UX/UI Designer from day one, I was responsible for the full arc: understanding user needs, defining requirements, managing stakeholders, and designing the product. The project also hit a significant constraint mid-way: a budget cut forced a complete pivot from a custom build to adapting an existing platform, BISO. Navigating that change while keeping users and stakeholders aligned was the defining challenge of the project.

The end result was a redesigned, tailored CRM used by 20–50 employees across multiple locations — replacing a system that users had long worked around rather than with.

## Project Overview

**Organisation:** Ask! Beratungsdienste — a Swiss organisation offering career counselling and youth psychological services  
**Role:** UX/UI Designer & Product Owner  
**Timeline:** October 2020 – April 2022

## Research & Discovery

The existing system had been in use for years without meaningful updates. Users had developed workarounds for basic tasks, and satisfaction was low. To understand the actual workflows — not just the documented ones — I combined observation, interviews, and surveys. The gap between how the system was supposed to work and how people actually used it became the foundation for everything that followed.

From this I developed three user personas that grounded design decisions throughout the project and gave the development team a shared reference point for priorities. I also ran workshops to map system context and stakeholder priorities — translating research findings into a structured product direction the whole team could work from.

Holding both the PO and design role meant keeping two perspectives in constant tension: what users needed, and what was realistic to build. That tension sharpened every prioritisation decision — particularly after the budget pivot forced a hard reset on scope.

## Employee Co-Design

Participation mattered to the Ask! team. Rather than presenting a finished direction, I ran a series of polls that let employees contribute to design decisions progressively — first on overall style direction, later on typography and colour. The results were concrete: 70% preferred a modern aesthetic, rounded elements consistently outperformed sharp ones, and card-based layouts were explicitly called out as clear and readable. Dark mode was ruled out almost unanimously.

This wasn't just engagement for its own sake — it gave me real signal on preferences and meant the team felt ownership over the outcome before it was built.

## Platform Pivot & Redesign

A budget cut mid-project forced a shift from a custom build to adapting BISO, an existing platform used across several Swiss cantons. Before committing to the redesign direction, I ran usability tests on BISO in its current state to identify the biggest pain points and decide what to carry forward.

I built a design system first, which made it possible to redesign the necessary screens efficiently. A/B tests with the focus group resolved open questions on layout. The final visual direction was tailored to the organisation's CI guidelines and shaped by the earlier employee polls.

## Testing & Iteration

Throughout development, a cross-departmental focus group stayed involved from the start — running short co-design workshops on each new feature area before moving to high-fidelity prototypes. This kept the work grounded in real use rather than assumptions.

Usability testing used moderated Think Aloud sessions, both in-person and remote, tied to each major release. Findings fed directly into the next iteration.

## Reflection

This project shaped how I think about design in constrained environments. The budget pivot was disruptive, but working through it — adapting the brief, resetting stakeholder expectations, finding a viable path forward — was a more useful education than a smooth project would have been. Holding the PO role alongside design also gave me an early understanding of how product decisions get made, and how design input lands differently when you're also accountable for the backlog.
