As the sole designer on a government platform redesign, I wasn't just responsible for the design — I was responsible for establishing that design had a place in the process at all. There was no existing UX practice on the project when I joined. By the time I left, there was one.

The platform serves both government employees and external professional users — a technically complex application with workflows that had been built around administrative logic rather than user needs. My role was to change that framing: understand what users actually needed to do, define how the application should be structured to support them, and bring stakeholders along through a process most of them hadn't worked with before.

## Project Overview

**Client:** Federal Office of Information Technology, Systems and Telecommunication (BIT)  
**Role:** UX/UI Designer — Sole designer on the project  
**Timeline:** November 2024 – June 2025

## Establishing UX in a Non-UX Environment

The heaviest lift on this project wasn't the design work — it was making the case for doing it properly. I introduced user-centred methods to a team that was accustomed to requirement lists and technical specs. That meant running stakeholder workshops not just to gather requirements, but to reframe requirements around user goals. It meant translating complex, process-heavy workflows into user flows and navigation structures that non-technical stakeholders could actually evaluate. And it meant doing this consistently enough that it became part of how the team worked, not a one-off exercise.

For a project where the instinct is to define the system first and design around it later, establishing that sequence in the other direction was a meaningful shift.

## Information Architecture & Workflows

The platform's existing logic was built for administrators. Extending it to external professional users required rethinking the underlying structure — not just the surface. I led workshops to map the system's requirements against actual user tasks, then translated that into a clear information architecture and navigation structure.

The core challenge was complexity: workflows with many states, user types with overlapping but distinct needs, and a codebase that constrained some design decisions from the start. User flows became the primary tool for aligning the team — making the intended experience explicit enough that product owners and developers could identify gaps and conflicts before they became build problems.

## Reflection

Design decisions had to be legible to non-designers, and every requirement had to be interrogated for its user impact before it was accepted. Working as the sole designer in a team of two product owners and five developers, the challenge wasn't only the design work — it was making the case for it, consistently enough that it stopped feeling like a case and became the way the team worked. Earning a seat at the table is one thing. Using it is another.
